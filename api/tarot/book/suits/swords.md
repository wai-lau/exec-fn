Swords are the suit of air — thought, language, struggle, the cutting clarity of the mind. Where Swords appear, the reading concerns mental life and the suffering that often comes with it: anxiety, decision, doubt, betrayal, the long aftermath of a hard truth. Pollack does not treat Swords as inherently dark; the same blade that wounds also cuts through illusion. The suit asks whether the querent's thinking is serving them or trapping them, and whether the truth they fear is in fact the truth they need. Swords' conflicts are mental, not physical — they take place in the interior, where words and thoughts and old certainties do their damage and their work.
