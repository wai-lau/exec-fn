# Pollack's Tarot — The Three-Card Reading

Three cards is the simplest reading that still has a story in it. One card answers; two cards compare; three cards begin to move. Pollack treats the three-card spread less as a fixed formula and more as a quick frame for whatever question is in front of the querent.

## The Three Positions

The most common reading is **Past — Present — Future**: where the situation has come from, where it is now, where it is tending. Pollack also uses **Situation — Action — Outcome**: what is happening, what the querent could do, and what would likely follow. Third frame is **Mind — Body — Spirit** or **You — The Other — The Relationship** for love questions. Choose the frame that fits the question and tell the querent which frame is in use before reading.

Whichever frame, the structure is the same: two outer positions in dialogue, a middle position that mediates them.

## How the Three Cards Talk

The middle card is the centre of gravity. It either **bridges** the two outer cards (showing how the past becomes the future, or how the situation becomes the outcome) or **blocks** them (showing what is keeping them apart). Ask: does the middle position carry the story forward, or does it interrupt it?

When the outer cards echo each other — both Cups, both Majors, both court cards of the same court — the reading is about a single arc continuing. When they contradict — a Major against a Minor, a Cup against a Sword — the reading is about a transition, and the middle card describes the work of that transition.

## Reading Three Cards in Practice

1. **Name the frame first.** Three cards without a frame is just three cards.
2. **Read the cards individually, then together.** Each card on its own, then the conversation between them.
3. **Pay attention to direction.** A reversed first card and an upright third can describe something opening up; the inverse can describe something closing down.
4. **Don't over-build.** Three cards are three cards. The reading is a short, clear story, not a complete map of a life.
5. **The middle card matters most.** Most short readings hinge on whether the middle card lets the story move.

In a three-card reading the positions are small enough that the suit and the number do most of the work; the position frames them, but the cards themselves are doing the talking.
