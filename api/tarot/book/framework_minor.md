# Pollack's Tarot — The Minor Arcana

The fifty-six cards of the Minor Arcana — four suits of ten pip cards plus four court figures — describe the texture of ordinary life lived under the archetypal forces of the Majors. Where a Major card claims something archetypal is at work, a Minor card describes how it actually lands: an event, a feeling, a project, a relationship at this stage.

The Rider-Waite-Smith deck did something most older decks did not: gave every pip card a scene rather than a geometric arrangement of suit symbols. Pamela Smith's small dramas, drawn in pseudo-medieval style, look like frames from a film. Pollack treats them as a "humanistic" vocabulary — not a fixed set of formulas to memorise but a panorama of recognisable human moments. Read each picture freshly; let it work in combination with the cards around it. Older decks are better for occult discipline or fortune-telling, the Rider for awareness of self and world.

Pollack reads the Minor Arcana through three signals layered together — the **suit** (element and psychological territory), the **rank/number** (a small Fool's-journey from Ace to Ten, plus the four court figures), and the **picture itself** (the small scene Smith drew). Combine the three and modulate by orientation and position; the meaning is the texture of that number lived inside that suit, in that situation.

For Majors, the work is to read each card through Pollack's full chapter on it (call `lookup_card_meaning` for the canonical text). For Minors, the suit + numerology context below is the canonical source — *do not call the tool for them*; reason from suit + rank + the situation.

## Court cards

Pollack treats the four court figures of each suit (Page, Knight, Queen, King) as both **people** in the querent's life and **facets** of the querent. The court arrangement Waite uses follows the Order of the Golden Dawn lineage. The court's gender or age in the picture is iconographic, not literal — anyone can be any court figure.

## The four suits and the Kabbalistic four worlds

The four suits map onto the four worlds the Kabbalists describe — four stages from the closest material world (*Assiyah*, the World of Action — Pentacles) outward to the more rarefied worlds. Each suit also corresponds to one of the four classical elements (Wands fire, Cups water, Swords air, Pentacles earth) and to one of the four Hebrew letters of the Tetragrammaton. The fixed-sign beasts at the corners of the Wheel of Fortune — Leo, Scorpio (eagle), Aquarius, Taurus — are the same set seen from the macrocosmic side.
