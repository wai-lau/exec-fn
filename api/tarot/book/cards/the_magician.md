# The Magician (I)

The Magician emerges directly from the Fool as the trickster-wizard — Merlin in his magus aspect, or the conjurer and juggler of earlier decks (Charles Williams once described him as a juggler tossing the stars and planets). Where the Fool carried the wand lightly over his shoulder, the Magician holds it consciously. He is consciousness, action and creation: the moment something is *made real* out of the potentials in life.

Look at Waite's image. He is not casting spells. One hand raised to heaven, one pointing to the green earth — a lightning rod. He opens himself upward to draw the spirit force down, then grounds it in physical reality. The four suit-emblems on his table are the tools and elements he works with. Around him are flowers, because the creative force only matters when it lands in the world.

Pollack's central teaching here is that the life-force is not gentle: it must be discharged. The religious worshipper trembling and shouting, the fan at a sports event, the artist running hot on a project, the priest at communion — all are filled with the same energy. We function best as channels for it. Hoard the feeling and it withers; release it into something real and the flow continues. The wand-up half is what people are drawn to, but the *real* magic is in the downward finger.

She also distinguishes the Western wizard (in his magic circle, defending himself from the demons) from the shaman, who lets the ego dissolve under the spirit and serves as a clean channel for the community. The same distinction applies to using tarot: study the symbols, learn the language, but never forget the real magic is in the images themselves.

**In a reading.** Awareness of a power in your life — spirit, excitement, beginnings. The first real step (after the Fool's leap). Will-power: the will unified and aimed at a goal. People who reliably get what they want usually just know what they want; the Magician's gift is consciousness of the energy already available.

**Reversed.** The channel is disrupted. Sometimes that is weakness or paralysis — the lethargic apathy of depression, the lack of will that leads to doing nothing. Sometimes it is power abused: a strong character bent on a destructive influence (psychic aggression, "black magic"). Sometimes it is mental disquiet — the lightning not grounded, the energy turned inward as anxiety or hallucination. The word *panic* means "possessed by Pan". The reversed Magician is not to be banished; the work is to turn it right side up by finding somewhere real for the fire to go.
