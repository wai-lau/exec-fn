# The Tower (XVI)

The standard reading: the Tower is the materialist conception of the universe, and the lightning is what comes for a life built on it. Pollack starts there and goes further. The violence on the card is not really from outside. The person who lives only for ego — wealth, fame, physical pleasure — without introspection or any sense of a wider spiritual order, builds a prison around themselves: grey, rock-bound, gold-crowned at the top. Inside, pressure builds as the unconscious strains at its bonds. Dreams turn disturbed, arguments and depression more common; if those manifestations are also repressed, the unconscious finds another way to erupt.

The eruption can look like external disaster — friends and family turning, work collapsing, violence around you. Pollack acknowledges that bad luck genuinely does cluster, but asks how much of it is long-neglected situations finally arriving at the moment we are most vulnerable. The drops of fire on each side of the Tower are shaped like the Hebrew letter *yod*, the first letter of God's name. They symbolise not anger but grace — the universe will not allow us to stay imprisoned in towers of illusion forever, and if we cannot break ourselves free peacefully, the forces of life will arrange the explosion. Some decks call this card *The House of the Devil*; others, *The House of God*. The Devil is God's shadow.

The deeper meaning. Embracing the Devil destabilises Temperance's calm; the psyche sets a violent course. Jung described consciousness as a dam holding the unconscious river. Temperance is a sluice. The Tower blows the dam. The price is high but no other path goes past the conscious barrier that separates life into opposites and screens out the raw spirit fire. Mystics across traditions say revelation arrives as lightning — Paul on the road to Damascus, Buddha under the Bo tree — never gradually, and never without years of preparation, which doesn't earn the bolt but does make us strong enough to survive it.

The Tower sits below the High Priestess: this is her veil being ripped away. The lightning recalls the Magician's wand. The two people falling — one in blue, one in red — are the polarity all the earlier cards mapped, overwhelmed at last by the unity. Count the *yod* drops and there are 22 (the trumps), split 10 and 12 — the Sumerians' worldly and zodiacal counting, both shown here as manifestations of one fire.

The image evokes Babel — the literal story explains why we speak so many languages, but in another reading the lightning is God speaking directly, replacing human language with the speech that the shamans report in trance (the languages of beasts and birds). 16 reduces to 7, the Chariot, whose Kabbalistic association is *speech*; the Tower's speech of God destroys in one moment the careful constructions of culture, language, consciousness, and returns us to the sea beneath the Wheel and the pool behind the Priestess's veil.

**In a reading.** Usually the surface meaning: a period of violent upheaval, literal or psychological. Long-established situations destroyed. Relationships breaking up in anger. Many querents recoil at the sight of it; the work is to remember that the explosion can liberate — pressure clearing — and can lead to a real beginning. Occasionally (the surrounding cards will tell you) it is the lightning of insight itself, replacing a narrow view with a wider one.

**Reversed.** A milder version. Storm still here, but partly held. Waite calls this "imprisonment" — the paradox being that the upright card liberates. Reversed, the querent has clamped down on their reactions; the pain is less but the repressed material has not been released. The experience continues inside, never running its course. By shielding the Tower from the lightning we become its prisoners.
