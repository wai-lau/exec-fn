# The Fool (0)

The Fool is the spirit free enough to step off the ordinary path. Pollack reads him as the leap into the archetypal world the Major Arcana describes — the figure willing to enter the strange landscape of magicians and dancers when the cautious would stay safely with job, home and family.

He is also the trickster: Merlin, the court jester at King Lear's side, the satirist, the King of Fools who presides over carnival. Cultures everywhere keep some version of this figure to needle the powerful back toward the truths they would rather ignore. The "Fool of God" tradition counted idiots, harmless madmen and severe epileptics among those touching a greater wisdom because they were out of touch with the rest of us.

In Waite's image the dog at his heels is the natural self in harmony with instinct; the white rose is passion lifted to a higher register; his black wand is unconscious power, infinite because no boundary has yet been set. The bag carries the eagle of soaring spirit — he carries experience without being ruled by it.

The Fool belongs anywhere in the deck, especially at difficult transitions between phases (Chariot to Strength, Temperance to the Devil) and on long passages like the Moon and Death. He combines Wands and Cups, action and imagination, fire and water — the alchemy of transformation.

In a reading he counsels courage, optimism, faith in the inner self over the practical pressure of others. He often marks beginnings taken from deep feeling rather than careful planning.

**Reversed.** The same instinct blocked or refused. Sometimes over-caution — failing to take a real chance from fear or over-reliance on others' advice. Sometimes the opposite-looking form: recklessness, wildness, crazy schemes — which spring from the same root, a refusal to listen inwardly. The Fool's deeper teaching is timing: great chances at the right moment, stillness when stillness is what is being asked for.
