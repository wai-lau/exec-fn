# The Sun (XIX)

Like the Hanged Man above it, the Sun is both a joyful release after the previous card's test and a preparation for the death-and-rebirth in the two cards that follow. Justice required active response, so the Hanged Man could be passive. The Moon required passive surrender (we can't control visions), so the Sun is active and energised. Accept the Moon's images and the energy comes out into ordinary life, giving everything a radiance.

Under the Sun everything turns simple, joyous, physical. The light of the unconscious carried into daily life. The two children in some older versions (one common gloss reads them as the eternal self and the mortal body) hold hands — joined together. The triangular motif first seen in the Hierophant returns here for the last time: the figures below, the Sun above, but the Sun now *joins* the inner and outer poles rather than mediating between them.

Pollack stresses that we are all children in the sun-religion sense — holy children of the light — but if you look at the adult bodies in some versions you'll see this is not literal childhood. The successful passage from the Tower has given the querent a childlike simplicity. The Tarot lays the passage out as stages — Tower, Star, Moon, Sun — but more often it happens all at once: blinding revelation, inner radiance, acute fear and the aftermath of joy compressed into a single transformative moment.

Enlightenment carries the same characteristics across cultures, however the doctrine glosses them. It is experience, not idea. A burst of light. The world felt as spiritual and eternal rather than the drudgery of day-to-day. A childlike joy that actual children probably never feel — because this querent has passed *through* the darkness rather than not yet meeting it. The sunstruck person becomes "lucid" — a word that literally means filled with light. Apollo, god of light, was born from Leto, goddess of night, and his oracle at Delphi originally belonged to dark goddesses; even his wisdom operated out of the dark.

The spring sun brings life out of dead winter ground; in many traditions the sun was believed to impregnate not only the soil but the embryo. The Gnostics and Kabbalists (Isaac Luria's *tikkun*) extended this: the divine light became scattered and imprisoned in individual bodies, and each person's work is to release the spark back toward unity. The sunstruck person sees this directly — every person, animal, plant, even the air, alive and holy, united through the light that fills existence. But the Sun is not yet the World. 19 *perceives* the universe as unified and alive; 21 will *embody* the perception.

Some decks show the children inside a walled garden, the "inner garden of the soul", a new Eden. Waite's version has one child riding *out* of a garden — for Waite the Sun was essentially liberation, a breaking loose from narrow consciousness. The two readings combine: once you realise the Garden of Eden lives within you, you can leave it freely and carry it with you. 19 is a higher 9 — the Hermit's lantern-light burst open into Abulafia's ecstatic third level; the bleak old man and snowy mountain were always illusions, the inner self emerges as a child. The other digit, 1, joins Magician energy to Hermit wisdom: super-consciousness, life-force united with meaning. 1+9 = 10, and where the Wheel was a vision of something outside, the Sun is the same vision from inside — no mysteries, no symbols, only the universe filled with light.

**In a reading.** Joy, happiness, a great sense of the beauty of life. At its deepest a wholly new way of seeing — all of life united in joy and light. Above all: optimism, energy, wonder.

**Reversed.** The good things are not lost but clouded over. Life is still giving the querent a time of simple happiness, but they cannot see it clearly. Not lucid yet — and they will have to work to realise the joy that is the Sun's actual gift.
