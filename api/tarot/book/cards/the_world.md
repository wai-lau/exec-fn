# The World (XXI)

What can be said of an understanding, a freedom and rapture beyond words? The unconscious known consciously, the outer self at one with the forces of life, knowledge that isn't knowledge so much as a constant ecstatic dance of being — all true and all not-true at once.

The number and the two wands unify the Magician and the High Priestess. The Wheel of Fortune foreshadowed the World — the same four creatures sit at its corners, the symbols of the Wheel now living. One way or another, the Wheel has come up for every card in the last row; the row's purpose is to unite the querent with everything trump 10 showed as external — fate, the workings of life, the elements of existence — and when the unity arrives the symbols dissolve into the dance.

The Hanged Man held the bliss through total inactivity. But even the World Tree was an illusion the mind needed to grasp. True unity, Pollack says, is found in motion. The Earth circles the sun, the sun moves through the galaxy, the galaxies cluster and turn. There is no fixed centre. Yet the centre is everywhere, because the dance carries its own peaceful centre wherever the dancer is. Nothing and everything at once.

And so we return to the Fool. Innocence and emptiness, now joined with wisdom. Of all the Major Arcana, only these two are moving. The oval wreath suggests 0, with all the Fool's symbolism, and it suggests the cosmic egg — all things in potential, all potentials realised. The red sashes tied as lemniscates at top and bottom say the self is not enclosed but open. Red is the kundalini root chakra: the dancer has not lost her physical, sexual reality. Green is the natural world raised; purple is divinity; blue is communication. When divinity is no longer something outside, your presence communicates it.

The card's closest analogue is Shiva, Lord of the Cosmic Dance — arms out, one foot planted in the physical, one foot raised in the soul's release; the face neither sad nor joyful but at peace. Shiva is often shown hermaphroditic (Shiva and Parvati in one body); the esoteric tradition reads the World dancer the same way, the dual sexual organs concealed by the banner because what they represent lies past our knowing. The Gnostics, Blake, the Kabbalah's *Adam Kadmon* — many traditions hold that the universe was once a single human being of pure light, broken up at the beginning so that the light became imprisoned in matter. (A curious modern echo: cosmology says the universe was originally one particle, all light before pieces condensed into mass.) Most myths consider the break-up irreversible. Occultists do not — the Major Arcana traces the restoration.

The dancer's posture is the Tree of Life. Crown and shoulders the top triangle (super-consciousness), hands and genitals the middle (consciousness), crossed leg and right foot the bottom (unconscious) — all one body. The tenth Sephira, the root of the Tree, is not in the body but in the ground of being everywhere around her.

**In a reading.** Success, achievement, satisfaction. The querent's inner sense of being unified with their outer activities, to whatever degree.

**Reversed.** Stagnation. The movement and growth slowed almost to a stop. Or so it seems. The freedom and rapture of the World exists always in potential, waiting to be released when the querent is ready to begin the dance again.

The card's real meanings are unknowable — a goal, a hope, an intuition. The way there is the music of the rest of the Major Arcana.
